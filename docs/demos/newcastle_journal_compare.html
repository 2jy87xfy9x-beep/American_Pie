Queen of all hearts

THIS is a great day. Once more we British make plain to the world that we hold firmly to our way of life.

And, most appropriately, last night brought great news to back the claim that our way usually takes us, in the end, to the top.

At the third attempt, the sixth British expedition, under Col. John Hunt, has conquered Everest. It is the happiest of omens.

Skill, tenacity, courage, and great endurance have overcome the legendary powerful defences of the mightiest mountain on earth.

Mr. E. P. Hillary He reached the summit.
Right instinct

THE significance of Everest may be clearer to the mass of our people than is the symbol of the Coronation ceremony.

But they know instinctively that the underlying concern is always for them; for their welfare, their protection, their rights and liberties.

They know, too, something which baffles other peoples; that the Crown has gained in influence far more than it has been surrendered in direct power.

Yet, ordinarily, they make no parade whatever of their patriotic devotion.

11th EXPEDITION
The Queen was wakened at Buckingham Palace late last night to be told that the British Expedition has conquered Mount Everest.

This great news, on the eve of the Coronation, reached London last night in a message to "The Times" from Col. John Hunt in charge of the expedition.

The climb was made on Friday and Col. Hunt has reported that "all is well."

The successful assault was made by Mr. E.P. Hillary, a New Zealander, and the Sherpa porter named Tensing Bhutia.

This great feat of the new Elizabethans flashed round the world adding still further joy to the heightening Coronation fever.

Mr. Hillary, aged 34, is a beekeeper in New Zealand. His climbing experience was gained in the Southern Alps in the South Island, a range that has attracted mountaineers from all over the world because of the difficulty of the climbs.

AN attractive girl of 16, found dead in the Thames at Richmond yesterday, was murdered and her body flung in the river near Teddington Lock on Sunday night, police decided after a post-mortem examination last night.

There was a gash on her forehead, but death was due to three stab wounds in the back. Blood-stains were found on the grassy and wooded verge of the river towpath near the lock a mile and a half away.

The dead girl was identified by her mother as Barbara Songhurst, of Princess Road, Teddington. She was an assistant at a chemist's shop.

On Sunday, she went out with a friend, Christine Reed (16), of Roy Crescent, Richmond Hill. Christine has not yet been reported as missing.

Police later feared that they had a double murder to contend with. Another cycle has been found.

Went Cycling

The dead girl was identified by her mother as Barbara Songhurst. On Sunday she went cycling with Christine Reed. They were last seen near Twickenham.

Duke will watch

The Duke of Edinburgh will attend the Coronation ceremony. He has been deeply involved in the preparations for the great occasion.

Duke will watch the Coronation from a seat in the Abbey, as a special guest of the Queen. He is expected to arrive from New Zealand before the ceremony.

Mr. Hillary will be flown back to Britain immediately to take part in Coronation celebrations. He will be knighted by the Queen.

The achievement comes on the eve of the greatest ceremony in the history of the monarchy. The timing could not have been more perfectly contrived to demonstrate the vigour and enterprise of the new Elizabethan age.

Col. Hunt, who led the expedition, is a soldier of wide experience. He has served in many theatres and brings to mountain climbing the same qualities of leadership and endurance that distinguish the finest of British soldiers.

The Sherpa porters of Mount Everest have at last been given their due recognition. Without their endurance and skill, no British expedition could have reached even the lower slopes.
