What Was Already Over Before Anyone Called It A Crisis
The official start date of the 2008 recession is December 2007. By then seven of the nine indicators used to determine that date had already been deteriorating for between four and eleven months.

The people who came out of it ahead were not the ones who predicted it. They just knew a number most people had never calculated.

In the news — Early 2008
An 85-year-old institution that survived the Great Depression, two world wars and the dot-com collapse did not survive the weekend. It sold for $2 a share. Twelve months earlier: $172. The Federal Reserve cut rates for the fourth time in six months. Your savings account already noticed. You probably haven't. The safety rating on the product at the center of the collapse was AAA. The firm that issued that rating was paid by the firm that created the product. Both firms are still operating. A man who had been warning about this specific scenario in writing since 2005 quietly made $100 million the week Bear Stearns collapsed. The warning was public. The trade was legal. Oil hits $100 a barrel. Something else also hit a record that week. It was in your wallet. Nobody was covering that story.

What Was Already Over Before Anyone Called It A Crisis

In January 2008 five households had roughly the same amount of money, roughly the same expenses, and roughly the same understanding of what was happening around them. Which is to say almost none.

What was happening was not hidden. The Federal Reserve's own published minutes described it with increasing urgency across six consecutive meetings. The largest banks in the country had stopped lending to each other — the financial equivalent of hospitals refusing to share blood. None of this was classified. All of it was public.

One of the five households was reading it.

The thing they were reading was not complicated. It required no finance degree, no brokerage account, no money beyond what was already sitting in a checking account.

It required knowing one number. Take everything liquid. Divide by what leaves the account in an average month — not income, what leaves. Multiply by thirty. The result is days.

For one household that number was 97 days▸. Calculated on a Tuesday. Nothing prompted it except mild unease about money that had been building since November.

The other four households had a feeling about this number. Fine, probably. The feeling was not a number. In a crisis a feeling spends like nothing at all.

▸ What the other four had instead of a number
The other four had between 23 and 61 days of runway — a fact none of them knew. They would find out. Not on a quiet Tuesday. In the middle of something else entirely, when the information arrived with no good options attached to it.

The Weekend An 85-Year-Old Institution Ceased To Exist

Bear Stearns had survived the Great Depression, World War II, the savings and loan crisis, and the dot-com collapse. It did not survive the weekend of March 14, 2008.

Its two internal hedge funds were holding approximately $20 billion in mortgage-backed securities — bundles of American home loans repackaged and sold as investment-grade assets. The credit rating on most of them was AAA. The firm that issued those ratings was paid by the firm that created the products.

By July 2007 both funds had lost essentially all of their value. The firm told clients the situation was contained. When something that size gets called contained, the correct response is to find out what that word is doing in that sentence. It was doing nothing. It was decoration.

The Meeting Notes That Were Always Public And Almost Never Read

On January 22 2008 the Federal Reserve cut its benchmark rate by 75 basis points in an emergency session. Largest single cut in 26 years. Not scheduled. Emergency sessions are rare. The Fed does not hold them for announcements that do not matter.

What it meant for the $7,000▸ sitting in the average household checking account: the rate on that account, already at 0.04 percent annually, would fall further. The $2.80▸ earned per year on that balance would become less.

Somewhere else — a high-yield account requiring only knowledge of its existence — the same amount was earning $315▸ a year. The distance between those two outcomes was one phone call and nineteen minutes. One household made it in January.
