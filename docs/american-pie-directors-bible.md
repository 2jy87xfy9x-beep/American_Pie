## 1. The Core Premise

The simulation is not a story about people. It is a story about **decisions and the variables that determine their outcomes.**

Five households enter 2008 with identical circumstances. They are not characters in the traditional sense. They are **vehicles** — containers through which decisions travel. The reader does not root for a household. The reader roots for a decision. Or fears one. Or watches one compound into something irreversible over eighteen years.

The reader is always the sixth vehicle. The one whose decisions have not been made yet.

This is what separates American Pie from every other financial education tool: **the lesson is never stated. It is felt. And it is felt because the reader is not watching someone else's story. They are watching the story of decisions that are available to them right now, today, playing out to their full conclusion.**

The horror is not fictional. The excitement is not vicarious. Both are real because the decisions are real.

## 2. The Seven Emotional Levers

These are the mechanisms that create emotional engagement throughout the simulation. Each lever is active during specific eras. The document maps when each one fires.

<details>
<summary><strong>Lever 1 — Dramatic Irony</strong></summary>

**What it is:**
The reader is in 2026 reading about January 2008. They know Lehman is going to collapse. They know the market will bottom on March 9 2009. They know the household that just added leverage is going to lose everything. The households do not know any of this.

**The feeling it produces:**
Quiet dread dressed as calm. The specific horror of watching someone walk toward a cliff they cannot see. One of the most gripping feelings in storytelling and the most underused in financial writing.

**When it is active:**
Every era. It never turns off. It is the ambient emotional temperature of the entire simulation.

**How to write it:**
Never state what is coming. Let the reader carry the knowledge alone. The gap between what the reader knows and what the household knows IS the emotion. Do not close that gap with explanation.

**What kills it:**
Foreshadowing. Any sentence that says or implies "little did they know." The reader already knows. Trust them.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Lever 2 — Allegiance Without Introduction</strong></summary>

**What it is:**
The reader develops genuine investment in certain decisions without being told to. This happens through repeated exposure — seeing the same decision patterns across multiple eras until the reader is tracking them without realizing it.

**The feeling it produces:**
The particular satisfaction of figuring something out yourself. The reader discovers the five vehicles the way you discover a recurring character in a novel — through pattern recognition, not exposition.

**When it is active:**
Begins Era 1. Fully established by Era 3.

**How to write it:**
Never introduce the vehicles. Never name them. Never describe them as characters. Let their decisions introduce them. The first time a reader expands a "See also" entry and recognizes the behavior from two eras ago — that is the moment allegiance forms. That moment must be earned, not engineered.

**What kills it:**
Any label. Any color code visible to the reader. Any sentence that says "one of the five households — the one who tends to make conservative decisions —". The moment you introduce them as characters they stop being decisions and start being people. The distance returns.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Lever 3 — Gleeful Complicity</strong></summary>

**What it is:**
The reader secretly roots for the decision to gamble and win. Not because gambling is smart. Because watching a decision that should not work and have it work anyway produces a very specific feeling — somewhere between joy and guilt — that is more educational than any warning label.

**The feeling it produces:**
Gleeful excitement with a guilty aftertaste. The reader enjoys the win. Then notices they enjoyed it. Then asks why. That asking is the lesson landing.

**When it is active:**
Era 3 through Era 5. Peaks in Era 4 when the December 2008 purchase is revealed to be correct.

**How to write it:**
Write the winning decision with the same dry precision as everything else. Do not editorialize. Do not add warnings. Let the number speak. The reader will feel the excitement without being told to. The guilt arrives on its own when the parallel losing decision is shown on the same screen.

**What kills it:**
Adding a disclaimer. Any sentence that says "of course, this strategy is extremely risky." The reader knows. The disclaimer kills the complicity and with it the lesson.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Lever 4 — Slow Motion Horror</strong></summary>

**What it is:**
The specific horror of watching nothing happen. Not a dramatic loss. Not a visible crisis. Just the quiet accumulation of absent decisions over eighteen years until the gap between what happened and what could have happened becomes impossible to ignore.

**The feeling it produces:**
The most durable horror in the simulation. It does not shock. It settles. It is still there weeks after the reader has finished.

**When it is active:**
Runs as a quiet undercurrent from Era 1. Surfaces fully in Era 5 when the reader sees what compound interest did to the correct decisions and what compound debt did to the absent ones. Arrives completely in Era 9.

**How to write it:**
Show the number. Show the date. Show what that number became. Never editorialize. The math is the horror. Your job is to get out of the way.

**What kills it:**
Pity. Any sentence that softens the landing with empathy for the household. The horror requires distance. The moment you humanize the absent decision it becomes a sad story about a person instead of a devastating story about a variable.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Lever 5 — Guilty Satisfaction At Watching Overconfidence Fail</strong></summary>

**What it is:**
The reader watches a decision that is analytically correct, confidently executed, and still catastrophically wrong because of one variable pulled too far. The reader feels satisfaction watching it fail. Then feels guilty about the satisfaction. Then realizes the guilt is the lesson.

**The feeling it produces:**
The specific pleasure of watching competence destroyed by overconfidence. It teaches more about leverage than any definition because it makes the reader feel the mechanism rather than understand it.

**When it is active:**
Era 3 through Era 5. The leverage decision in Era 3. The consequence in Era 4. The full cost in Era 5.

**How to write it:**
The losing decision and the winning decision must be written identically until the single variable that separates them is revealed. Same analysis. Same timing. Same confidence. One line of difference. That line is the character. That line is everything.

**What kills it:**
Writing the losing decision as obviously foolish in retrospect. The reader must feel that they might have made the same choice. The moment the decision looks stupid in hindsight the lesson evaporates.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Lever 6 — Procedural Satisfaction</strong></summary>

**What it is:**
The feeling of figuring something out yourself. Every time a "See also" entry clicks into place. Every time a variable updates and the story changes. Every time the reader realizes they have been tracking five decision patterns for three eras without being told to. The satisfaction is not the information. It is the act of assembling it.

**The feeling it produces:**
The addictive quality of a puzzle that does not announce itself as a puzzle. The reader is not solving something. They are reading. And then they realize they have been solving something for the past forty minutes.

**When it is active:**
Continuous. Every interactive element is a procedural satisfaction mechanism. The variable editor. The reveal block. The "See also" expansion. Every one of these should feel like a discovery, not a feature.

**How to write it:**
Design the information gaps deliberately. Decide what the reader needs to not know yet. Decide what they need to discover rather than be told. The interaction is the reward. The information inside it is secondary.

**What kills it:**
Telling the reader what they are about to discover. Any sentence that says "if you look at the margin you will see." The margin should just be there. The discovery should be the reader's.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Lever 7 — Personal Stakes Escalation</strong></summary>

**What it is:**
The simulation starts with other people's decisions. It ends with the reader's. The escalation is gradual and never announced. By Era 3 the reader has entered their own burn rate. By Era 5 they are calculating what their own 2009 investment would be worth today. By Era 9 the question is no longer what happened to five vehicles. The question is what is going to happen to the reader.

**The feeling it produces:**
The simulation has been a controlled environment for practicing a feeling the reader is about to have in real life. By the end they are not watching a story. They are rehearsing their own.

**When it is active:**
Begins the moment the reader taps their first variable. Escalates every time a real number replaces a mock number. Arrives fully in Era 9.

**How to write it:**
The variable editor is not a data entry tool. It is the mechanism by which the reader enters the simulation. Every time they replace a mock number with a real one they step one degree closer to the center of the story. Write the screens around real numbers with slightly more intimacy than the screens around mock numbers. Not much. Just enough to feel different.

**What kills it:**
Forcing it. Any screen that explicitly invites the reader to "apply this to your own life." The escalation must feel accidental. The reader must feel like they chose to enter the simulation, not like they were guided there.

</details>

## 3. The Variable Characters

These are the true characters of the simulation. Not people. Variables. Each one has an emotional function. Each one plays a role in the plot. Each one has a relationship with the reader that changes across eras.

<details>
<summary><strong>coverage_days — The Protagonist</strong></summary>

**What it represents:**
The number of days a household can survive without income. Calculated from cash and monthly expenses.

**Emotional function:**
Safety when high. Dread when low. The reader's relationship with this number is the emotional spine of Era 1 through Era 4. It is the first number the reader calculates for themselves. It is the number that makes the simulation personal.

**Character arc:**
Introduced as an abstract concept. Becomes personal the moment the reader enters their own numbers. Becomes urgent in Era 3 when the clock starts. Becomes the difference between surviving and not surviving by Era 4.

**How to write it:**
Always show it as a number of days, not months. Days are visceral. Months are abstract. "97 days" creates tension. "3.2 months" does not.

**The moment it lands:**
When the reader calculates their own coverage days for the first time and the number is lower than they expected. That moment is the simulation working exactly as intended.

**Relationship to other variables:**
`coverage_days` = `your_cash` ÷ `your_monthly_expenses` × 30. It is entirely dependent on two other variables. Its vulnerability is the vulnerability of everything that feeds it.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>fed_funds_rate — The Invisible Villain</strong></summary>

**What it represents:**
The Federal Reserve's benchmark interest rate. Moves seven times between September 2007 and April 2008.

**Emotional function:**
The villain that has no face. It moves and ordinary people's savings accounts earn less and nobody sends them a letter. The specific anger this produces — at a mechanism rather than a person — is one of the most important emotional experiences in the simulation.

**Character arc:**
Introduced as a number on a chart. Becomes personal when the reader sees what $7,000 earns at 0.04% versus 4.5%. Becomes sinister in Era 2 when the emergency cuts reveal that the people making these decisions knew exactly how bad things were. Becomes instructive in Era 8 when the same mechanism works in the reader's favor.

**How to write it:**
Show the rate table. Show the dates. Show the cuts. Let the reader count them. Seven cuts in seven months is a pattern that produces dread without any editorial comment. The pattern is the character.

**The moment it lands:**
When the reader sees $2.80 and $315 side by side and realizes the only difference was knowing one account existed. The villain in that moment is not the Fed. It is the information gap. That is the real villain of this simulation.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>leverage — The Antagonist</strong></summary>

**What it represents:**
Borrowed money used to amplify a position. The single variable that separates the two gambler vehicles.

**Emotional function:**
The most dramatic variable in the simulation. The same decision, the same analysis, the same timing — one variable separating triumph from ruin. Leverage is not good or bad. It is an amplifier. It amplifies whatever is already happening. In Era 3 what is already happening is a correct thesis about to become a catastrophically over-concentrated position.

**Character arc:**
Does not appear until Era 3. Arrives quietly — one line in a "See also" entry. "Added leverage Thursday." The reader may not catch it the first time. They will catch it when the consequence arrives in Era 4.

**How to write it:**
Never explain leverage before the reader feels it. Let the consequence arrive first. The explanation becomes meaningful because the feeling is already there.

**The moment it lands:**
Era 4. The market bounces unexpectedly. One position is catastrophically wrong. The other identical position is fine. One variable. That moment is the most efficient financial lesson in the simulation. It teaches leverage better than any definition because the reader feels the mechanism through the gap between two outcomes.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>time — The Dual Character</strong></summary>

**What it represents:**
The number of years between a decision and its full consequence. The variable that is simultaneously the greatest ally and the greatest enemy depending entirely on what the other variables are doing.

**Emotional function:**
Compound interest with the right variables. Compound debt with the wrong ones. The same force. Opposite outcomes. The reader experiences both simultaneously by tracking multiple vehicles across eighteen years.

**Character arc:**
Invisible in Era 1. Barely visible in Era 2. Starts to show its face in Era 5 when the reader sees what four years of correct decisions looks like. Arrives fully in Era 9 when the reader sees eighteen years of divergence from an identical starting point.

**How to write it:**
Use specific dates. Not "over the next few years." Exact dates. The specificity makes time feel like an active force rather than a passive backdrop. "By March 2013 — five years and two weeks after the calculation on that Tuesday — the $1,000 invested at the bottom was worth $3,200" is a different sentence than "the investment grew significantly over the following years."

**The moment it lands:**
Era 9. The final comparison. Same starting point. Eighteen years. Five completely different outcomes. Time did not cause the difference. But time made it irreversible.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>burn_rate — The Revealer</strong></summary>

**What it represents:**
Total monthly outgoing. Not income. What leaves.

**Emotional function:**
The variable that reveals what people actually do with money as opposed to what they think they do. Almost every household that calculates their burn rate for the first time discovers it is higher than expected. That discovery is small and private and one of the most significant financial moments a person can have.

**Character arc:**
Introduced in Era 1 as a concept. Becomes personal immediately — it is the first variable the reader is invited to calculate for themselves. Becomes urgent in Era 3 when it determines how long the money lasts. Becomes a habit by Era 5 for the vehicles that survive.

**How to write it:**
Write the calculation out fully every time. Not as a formula. As a story. "Take everything that left the account in October. Add November. Add December. Divide by three. That number is what your life actually costs per month." The calculation is the character. Let it breathe.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>opportunity_cost — The Ghost</strong></summary>

**What it represents:**
What a decision that was not made would have returned. The cost of inaction.

**Emotional function:**
The ghost that haunts every screen without appearing on it. The reader is always aware — because they are in 2026 — of what the money that sat in checking could have become. That awareness is a form of grief that the reader carries through the entire simulation.

**Character arc:**
Never introduced by name in the early eras. Felt as a quiet presence. Named explicitly only in Era 4 when the opportunity is at its peak and most vehicles are not taking it. By the time the reader encounters the word "opportunity cost" in the simulation they already know what it feels like.

**How to write it:**
Show the number the money became. Never the number the money could have become. The reader will do that calculation themselves. Their version will be more devastating than anything you could write.

</details>

## 4. The Decision Characters

Decisions are the plot events of the simulation. Each one below is a named character — not a person, not a household, but a decision — with an emotional function and a moment where it either arrives or fails to arrive.

<details>
<summary><strong>The Calculation — The Inciting Incident</strong></summary>

**The decision:**
To calculate coverage days before needing to know them.

**When it appears:**
Era 1. One household. A Tuesday evening. Nothing prompted it except mild unease.

**Emotional function:**
The decision that makes every other decision possible. Without knowing the number you are reacting. With it you are deciding. The entire simulation flows from whether this decision was made before or after the crisis arrived.

**How to write it:**
The act of calculation must feel small. Eleven minutes. A Tuesday. No drama. The drama is in what the number enables downstream — not in the moment of calculation itself. The smallness of the act against the scale of its consequence is the emotional content.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>The Move — The Quiet Win</strong></summary>

**The decision:**
To move savings from checking to a high-yield account.

**When it appears:**
Era 1. One household. One phone call. Nineteen minutes.

**Emotional function:**
Gleeful excitement at a win that required nothing except knowing the win was available. The reader should feel the specific pleasure of watching a small correct decision compound quietly over eighteen years. No drama. No insight. Just knowing one thing most people did not know.

**How to write it:**
Write it small. One line in a "See also" entry the first time. The reader should almost miss it. Its significance only becomes clear retroactively — when the $315 a year turns into something real over time.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>The Delay — The Decision That Was Not Made</strong></summary>

**The decision:**
To not file for unemployment immediately because it felt embarrassing.

**When it appears:**
Era 3. One household. Twenty-three days. $1,260 in benefits not received.

**Emotional function:**
The most human decision in the simulation and the most devastating to watch. Not a bad analysis. Not overconfidence. Just a feeling — embarrassment — overriding a practical action for three weeks. The reader should feel the cost of that feeling precisely.

**How to write it:**
Name the feeling. "Reason given to self: not ready." Four words. The reader fills in the rest from their own experience. Do not explain why the feeling was wrong. The $1,260 explains it.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>The Add — The Point Of No Return</strong></summary>

**The decision:**
To add leverage to a winning position.

**When it appears:**
Era 3. One household. One line. "Added leverage Thursday." The thesis was correct. The position was already profitable. The decision to amplify it is the decision that creates the difference between the two gamblers.

**Emotional function:**
The decision the reader will replay. Not because it was obviously wrong — it was not obviously wrong. Because it was unnecessary. The position was already working. The add was greed wearing the costume of conviction. The reader should feel the specific quality of that decision — how it would have felt correct in the moment — and then watch it become the variable that changes everything.

**How to write it:**
Write it identically to the decision not to add leverage. Same screen. Same position. Same numbers. One line of difference. "Added leverage Thursday." versus "Position held." Let the reader sit with how similar those two lines look before the consequence arrives.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>The Buy — The Most Frightening Correct Decision</strong></summary>

**The decision:**
To buy an S&P 500 index fund on March 9 2009. The actual market bottom. The best single buying opportunity in modern history. At the moment it felt most insane to do it.

**When it appears:**
Era 4. One household. $1,000. At a moment when every instinct said do not.

**Emotional function:**
The reader knows this is the bottom. The household does not. Watching a correct decision get made for the right reasons at the exact right time — not because of prediction but because of process — is the emotional peak of the simulation's first half. The reader should feel vicarious terror at the decision and vicarious triumph when it pays off.

**How to write it:**
Write the fear first. What the news was saying that week. What every signal was indicating. Then the decision. Then nothing — the reader has to wait until Era 5 to see what it became. The wait is part of the emotional content.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>The Sell — The Locked-In Loss</strong></summary>

**The decision:**
To sell everything in March 2020 at the bottom of the COVID crash.

**When it appears:**
Era 7. One household. Permanent.

**Emotional function:**
The reader has been watching this household for twelve eras. They know what is about to happen. The household does not. The decision locks in every loss from the fastest crash in history and removes the household from the fastest recovery in history simultaneously. The reader should feel helpless watching it happen. That helplessness is the lesson about panic selling landing.

**How to write it:**
One line. "Sold everything March 16." Do not describe the feeling that prompted it. The reader already knows the feeling. They have felt it. That recognition is the entire point.

</details>

## 5. The Five Vehicles

These are not characters. They are outcome containers — five possible trajectories through the same eighteen years. The reader encounters them through "See also" entries and the ticker. They are never introduced. They are discovered.

Each vehicle has an emotional function. Write to that function consistently. Never drift from it.

<details>
<summary><strong>Vehicle 1 — The Early Mover</strong></summary>

**Emotional function:**
Produces quiet satisfaction. Not excitement. Not inspiration. Just the specific feeling of watching boring correct decisions compound into something significant over time. The reader should feel the weight of each small action — not as a highlight but as a pattern. The pattern is the character.

**Tone when writing this vehicle:**
Dry. Factual. Slightly anticlimactic in the moment. The drama is always deferred to a later era. In Era 1 moving the savings account is one line. In Era 9 that line is worth $280,000 in compounded difference. Write Era 1 like it is one line. The reader will remember it in Era 9.

**What to never write:**
Any sentence that celebrates this vehicle in the moment. No "smart move." No "this would prove to be the right call." The dryness is the character. The celebration belongs entirely to the reader, in Era 9, when they do the math themselves.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Vehicle 2 — The Steady One</strong></summary>

**Emotional function:**
Produces the most underrated feeling in the simulation — the deep satisfaction of watching someone survive something that destroys people around them through nothing more than not doing anything catastrophically wrong. The reader should feel genuine affection for this vehicle by Era 3. Not because it is impressive. Because it is recognizable.

**Tone when writing this vehicle:**
Calm. Slightly boring. Never dramatic. The drama is always happening to others. This vehicle watches from a stable position and does not move. Write that stillness as a choice, not a default. "Did not act" is different from "did nothing." The distinction is everything.

**What to never write:**
Any suggestion that this vehicle missed something by being cautious. It did not miss anything. It survived. Survival is valid. Write it as valid.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Vehicle 3 — The Absent Decider</strong></summary>

**Emotional function:**
Produces slow motion horror. Not through dramatic failure but through the accumulation of absent decisions. The reader should not feel pity for this vehicle. They should feel the specific discomfort of recognition — the awareness that this vehicle's decisions are the default. What most people do. What the reader may be doing right now.

**Tone when writing this vehicle:**
Neutral. Clinical. Never pitying. The horror requires distance. Write the absent decisions with the same precision as the present ones. "Did not calculate burn rate" is written the same way as "calculated burn rate." The difference in outcome is the editorial comment. You do not need another one.

**What to never write:**
Any humanizing detail. No reasons given for the absent decisions beyond the one true reason — the information was not present. "Did not know the account existed." "Did not know the filing window." "Did not know the number." The information gap is the villain. Not the household.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Vehicle 4 — The Overreacher</strong></summary>

**Emotional function:**
Produces guilty satisfaction when the overreach fails. The reader watches a vehicle with genuine analytical skill, correct thesis, and real conviction make one decision too many and lose everything. The satisfaction is real. The guilt about the satisfaction is real. Both are the lesson.

**Tone when writing this vehicle:**
Write its analysis with respect. This vehicle is not foolish. Its thesis is correct. Its reading of the market is accurate. Write that clearly. The single variable that destroys it — leverage — must look like a reasonable extension of a working strategy, not an obvious mistake. The reader must feel that they might have made the same choice. That feeling is the entire emotional content of this vehicle.

**What to never write:**
Any early signal that this vehicle is going to lose. The dramatic irony is the reader's to carry. Do not share it with the text.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Vehicle 5 — The Lucky Right</strong></summary>

**Emotional function:**
Produces gleeful excitement followed by the most important admission in the simulation. Same decisions as Vehicle 4. Same analysis. Same timing. One variable different — no leverage. The outcome is $1.2 million versus negative $40,000. The vehicle's own admission in Era 9 that luck played a role is the moment the simulation's deepest lesson lands.

**Tone when writing this vehicle:**
Identical to Vehicle 4 until the leverage decision. After that point — slightly warmer. Not celebratory. Just the specific quality of someone who knows they are right and also knows that being right required something outside their control. Write that awareness into every entry from Era 4 onward.

**What to never write:**
Any suggestion that this vehicle's outcome was inevitable or deserved. It was not inevitable. It was correct analysis plus one variable of luck plus the self-awareness to not pretend the luck was skill. That combination is the rarest thing in the simulation.

</details>

## 6. The Emotional Arc — Era By Era

One line of emotional temperature per era. The dominant lever active. The reader's state at the end of each era. Use this to calibrate every screen beat you write.

<details>
<summary><strong>Era 1 — Quiet Dread Dressed As Calm (Late 2007 – Early 2008)</strong></summary>

**Temperature:** Low. Deceptively still.

**Dominant lever:** Dramatic irony. The reader knows what is coming. Nothing in the text acknowledges it.

**What the reader should feel entering:** Mild curiosity. The clickbait has worked.

**What the reader should feel leaving:** The first hint of unease. Something is building. They cannot name it yet.

**Key variable:** `coverage_days` — introduced but not yet personal.

**Key decision:** The Calculation. Made quietly. No drama.

**Writing note:** Resist the urge to hint at what is coming. The reader already knows. The calm is more effective than any foreshadowing.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Era 2 — The Beginning Of Allegiance (March 2008 – September 2008)</strong></summary>

**Temperature:** Warming. The irony sharpens.

**Dominant lever:** Allegiance without introduction. The reader starts recognizing decision patterns.

**What the reader should feel entering:** Engaged. Starting to track the vehicles without realizing it.

**What the reader should feel leaving:** The first genuine investment in an outcome. The reader has a preference. They may not have named it yet.

**Key variable:** `fed_funds_rate` — the invisible villain arrives.

**Key decision:** The Move. One phone call. Nineteen minutes. The reader should feel the smallness of it against the scale of what it will become.

**Writing note:** This is where the "See also" entries start gaining dates and context. The reader is building a mental model of the five vehicles. Do not accelerate this. Let it happen at the pace of recognition.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Era 3 — Helpless Witnessing (September 2008 – December 2008)</strong></summary>

**Temperature:** High. Multiple emotional registers firing simultaneously.

**Dominant lever:** All seven levers active simultaneously. This is the emotional peak of the first half.

**What the reader should feel entering:** Dread with edge. They know Lehman is coming.

**What the reader should feel leaving:** Emotionally full. They have felt triumph and horror and complicity and recognition all within the same era. They cannot stop reading.

**Key variable:** `leverage` — arrives quietly. "Added leverage Thursday."

**Key decisions:** The Delay. The Add. Both present. Both devastating in different ways.

**Writing note:** The five vehicles react to the same event in the same era for the first time. The reader gets to see the divergence in real time. Write each "See also" entry for this era with the same date stamp — September 15 2008 — and let the contrast do the work.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Era 4 — The Agony Of Knowing (January 2009 – March 2009)</strong></summary>

**Temperature:** Intense. The reader is carrying knowledge the households do not have.

**Dominant lever:** Dramatic irony at maximum. The reader knows this is the bottom. The households do not.

**What the reader should feel entering:** The weight of what they know.

**What the reader should feel leaving:** A specific kind of emotional exhaustion — the feeling of having watched something important happen and understood exactly what it meant and been unable to change it. That exhaustion is the lesson about market timing landing.

**Key variable:** `time` — starts to reveal itself. The Buy decision made here will define outcomes for the next fifteen years.

**Key decision:** The Buy. Terrifying and correct. The reader should feel both simultaneously.

**Writing note:** Do not reveal what the $1,000 became immediately. Make the reader wait until Era 5. The wait is emotional content.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Era 5 — The Pleasure Of Patience (2009 – 2013)</strong></summary>

**Temperature:** Warm. Earned.

**Dominant lever:** Procedural satisfaction. The correct decisions are starting to pay off. The reader has been waiting for this.

**What the reader should feel entering:** Relief. The worst is over.

**What the reader should feel leaving:** The specific satisfaction of watching patience rewarded. And underneath it — the ghost of opportunity cost for the vehicles that did not act.

**Key variable:** `compound_interest` — the friendly version of `time`. Arrives fully.

**Key variable:** `compound_debt` — the hostile version of the same force. Arrives for the vehicles carrying credit card balances.

**Writing note:** Show both simultaneously. The same variable. Opposite outcomes. The reader should feel the elegance and the horror of that duality without it being explained.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Era 6 — Gleeful Dread (2013 – 2019)</strong></summary>

**Temperature:** Deceptively warm. The dramatic irony returns in full.

**Dominant lever:** Gleeful dread. Everything is fine. The reader knows it will not stay fine. The specific pleasure of watching people be happy inside a story you know is about to change.

**What the reader should feel entering:** Relaxed. The crisis feels distant.

**What the reader should feel leaving:** Unsettled again. Bitcoin appeared. December 2019 appeared. Something is building.

**Key variable:** `inflation` — the slow silent background variable. Starts to matter.

**Key decision:** The Bitcoin purchase. Gleefully bad timing. The reader sees it coming.

**Writing note:** The one-line news item in December 2019 — "A novel pneumonia-like illness has been identified in Wuhan, China" — is the most important line in this era. Write everything else in this era to make that line land like a trapdoor.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Era 7 — Action With Personal Stakes (February 2020 – April 2020)</strong></summary>

**Temperature:** Urgent. Fast.

**Dominant lever:** Personal stakes escalation. By this point the reader has entered real numbers. What happens to the vehicles is happening to the reader's numbers.

**What the reader should feel entering:** The specific anxiety of a situation moving faster than understanding.

**What the reader should feel leaving:** The Sell decision has happened. The reader watched it. The reader had a feeling about it. That feeling is more valuable than any explanation of why panic selling is destructive.

**Key variable:** `time` — hostile again. The speed of this crash means decisions made in days have consequences that last years.

**Key decision:** The Sell. One line. Permanent.

**Writing note:** This era should feel faster than the others. Shorter screen beats. Less context per beat. The speed is part of the emotional content.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Era 8 — Slow Burn Anger (2021 – 2022)</strong></summary>

**Temperature:** Simmering.

**Dominant lever:** Slow motion horror — but this time the horror has a mechanism rather than a person. The reader should feel genuine low-grade anger at inflation as a force. Not at any decision. At the variable itself.

**What the reader should feel entering:** The post-COVID relief starting to curdle.

**What the reader should feel leaving:** Anger at a system and also — if the reader entered real numbers — a personal version of that anger. Their purchasing power fell too. The simulation is not historical anymore. It arrived in the present.

**Key variable:** `inflation_rate` — the variable the reader has been living through. Becomes personal immediately.

**Writing note:** This era is where the simulation and the reader's real life overlap most directly. Write it accordingly. The variables here are not historical. They are current.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Era 9 — Quiet Determination (2023 – 2026)</strong></summary>

**Temperature:** Still. Clear.

**Dominant lever:** Personal stakes at maximum. The simulation is ending. The reader is beginning.

**What the reader should feel entering:** The weight of eighteen years of other people's decisions.

**What the reader should feel leaving:** Not inspired. Not motivated. Just — clear. The reader knows what the variables are. They know what the decisions cost. They know what time does to both. They are not reading about finance anymore. They are holding it.

**Key moment:** Vehicle 5's admission. "Luck played a role. I know it." The most honest line in the simulation and the one that earns everything that came before it.

**Writing note:** The final screen is one paragraph. No fanfare. No score. Just: the simulation is complete. The real app is waiting. Your numbers are different. The concepts are the same. Let the reader sit with that.

</details>

## 7. The Three Tones

Every screen in the simulation uses one of these three tones or a deliberate blend of two. Read this section before writing any new screen beat. Identify the tone. Then execute it without drift.

<details>
<summary><strong>Tone 1 — The Document</strong></summary>

**What it sounds like:**
A Federal Reserve meeting summary. Precise. Institutional. No adjectives that are not measurements. No emotional language. The facts are allowed to be devastating. The writing is not allowed to notice.

**When to use it:**
Variable introductions. Rate tables. The moment a decision is made. Any screen where the reader needs to feel the weight of information without being told how to feel about it.

**Good example:**
*On January 22 2008 the Federal Reserve cut its benchmark rate by 75 basis points in an emergency session. It was the largest single cut in 26 years. It was not scheduled.*

**Bad example:**
*The shocking emergency rate cut sent tremors through financial markets as the Fed desperately tried to shore up a crumbling economy.*

**What drift looks like:**
Adjectives that editorialise. "Shocking." "Desperate." "Crumbling." The moment a tone-1 screen contains an adjective that belongs in a newspaper headline it has drifted into tone 2. Pull it back.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Tone 2 — The Long Read</strong></summary>

**What it sounds like:**
A New Yorker financial piece. Still rigorous. Still factual. But the writing has a current running underneath it. A sentence that ends somewhere unexpected. A detail that lands slightly harder than it needed to. The reader does not feel they are being written at. They feel they are being told something the writer thinks they should know.

**When to use it:**
The story beats that bridge events. The "See also" entries in the middle eras. Any screen where the reader needs context that could feel like a lesson if written wrong.

**Good example:**
*Bear Stearns had survived the Great Depression, World War II, the savings and loan crisis, and the dot-com collapse. It did not survive the weekend of March 14, 2008. The mechanism that brought it down had been accumulating inside the financial system for years. It was not a secret.*

**Bad example:**
*Sarah sat at her kitchen table and thought about what the collapse of Bear Stearns meant for ordinary Americans like her. She didn't know it then, but her life was about to change forever.*

**What drift looks like:**
Characters. Feelings attributed to people. "Thought about." "Felt." "Didn't know it then." The moment tone 2 grows a person inside it the simulation has become fiction. The reader exits. Pull it back to decisions and variables.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Tone 3 — The Revelation</strong></summary>

**What it sounds like:**
The moment in a documentary when the interview subject says the thing they were not supposed to say. Brief. Precise. Slightly uncomfortable. Sits on the screen a beat longer than the reader expects. Then the next screen loads.

**When to use it:**
The "Did You Know" hooks. The "On This Day" strip. The final line of a "See also" entry after it has been expanded. The reveal block. Any moment designed to produce the specific feeling of learning something that recontextualizes everything before it.

**Good example:**
*The CEO had told shareholders the firm was fine five days earlier. He was not charged with anything.*

**Bad example:**
*In a shocking display of corporate negligence that would later outrage millions of Americans, the CEO of Bear Stearns made statements about the firm's stability that proved to be catastrophically misleading.*

**What drift looks like:**
Outrage performed by the writing rather than produced in the reader. "Shocking." "Outrage." "Catastrophically misleading." Tone 3 works because it says the fact and stops. The moment the writing editorialises the reader's outrage is replaced by the writer's. Pull it back.

</details>

## 8. The Four Emotional Registers

These are the four primary feelings the simulation must produce. Each one has a technique for creating it in dry factual prose and a specific thing that kills it.

<details>
<summary><strong>Intrigue — The Need To Know What Happens Next</strong></summary>

**The technique:**
Withholding. Give the reader enough to need the next sentence. Not a cliffhanger. Not a twist. Just information delivered in an order that makes the next piece necessary.

**The rule:**
Every screen should end with something unresolved. Not a question. Not a dramatic pause. Just one element that is not yet explained. The reader closes the screen carrying that element. It makes the next screen feel necessary rather than optional.

**What kills it:**
Explaining too much on one screen. The moment the reader has everything they need the intrigue is gone. Distribute information across screens deliberately. Decide what the reader needs to not know yet.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Suspense — The Dread Of What Is Already Coming</strong></summary>

**The technique:**
Dramatic irony deployed with restraint. The reader knows what is coming. The writing does not acknowledge it. The gap between the reader's knowledge and the text's calm is the suspense. It requires the writing to maintain absolute composure while the reader is feeling something entirely different.

**The rule:**
Never break the composure. Not once. Not even at the moment the known thing arrives. Write Lehman's collapse with the same tone as a rate cut. The contrast between the scale of the event and the flatness of the prose is the technique. The flatness IS the suspense.

**What kills it:**
Any sentence that matches the reader's emotional state. "Markets were in freefall." "Panic spread through trading floors." The moment the prose becomes dramatic the suspense collapses into conventional storytelling. The reader exits.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Horror — The Cost Of The Absent Decision</strong></summary>

**The technique:**
Showing the number. Then showing what the number became. Then showing the corresponding number for the vehicle that did not act. No comment. No comparison made explicitly. Let the reader do the subtraction.

**The rule:**
Never pity the vehicle. Pity creates distance and with distance the horror becomes someone else's story. The horror must feel like the reader's potential story. Distance kills that. Write the absent decision with the same precision as the present one.

**What kills it:**
Reasons. Any explanation for why the decision was not made beyond "the information was not present." The moment a reason appears the horror becomes a lesson about a specific mistake rather than a feeling about an information gap. The feeling is more durable than the lesson.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Action — The Feeling Of Real Time Consequence</strong></summary>

**The technique:**
Compression. Short sentences. Specific dates. Numbers that move. Era 7 should feel faster than Era 1 in the writing itself — not through description of speed but through the pace of information delivery.

**The rule:**
Action in this simulation is not physical. It is decisional. A decision made in hours with consequences measured in years is action. Write the compression of time into the sentence structure. Short. Specific. No subordinate clauses.

**What kills it:**
Context that slows the pace. Era 7 screens should have less setup than Era 1 screens. The reader has been in the simulation for six eras. They do not need context. They need the decision and the immediate consequence. Give them both fast.

</details>

## 9. The Start — The Reader's Contract

The first five screens of the simulation establish a contract with the reader. This contract must never be broken. These are its terms.

<details>
<summary><strong>Term 1 — The Reader Will Never Be Lectured</strong></summary>

No screen will explain what the reader should think or feel. No screen will tell the reader what a concept means before the reader has felt why it matters. The concept follows the feeling. Always.

**How to honor it:**
Show the consequence before the cause. Show the number before the mechanism. Let the reader ask "why" before answering it.

**How to break it:**
Any sentence that begins with a definition. "A burn rate is the total monthly outflow of a household." Start instead with "The number was $2,100. It took eleven minutes to calculate. Most people have never calculated it." The reader will ask what the number is. Then tell them.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Term 2 — The Story Will Not Announce Its Transitions</strong></summary>

The simulation moves from static Wikipedia page to living financial app without ever saying it is doing so. No component introduces itself. No feature is revealed. Things simply become alive. The reader notices. The simulation does not.

**How to honor it:**
The ticker becomes live between screens without comment. The "See also" entries gain data without announcement. The margin editor appears when a variable is tapped without explanation.

**How to break it:**
Any instructional text. "You can now edit this value." "Notice that the ticker has updated." "The following section is interactive." None of these sentences exist in this simulation.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Term 3 — The Reader Will Always Know Where They Are In Time</strong></summary>

Every screen has a date. Not a section number. Not a progress bar. A date. The reader always knows when they are in the story. The date is the orientation.

**How to honor it:**
Date stamps in the "See also" entries. Era markers in the breadcrumb. The simulation date in the newspaper theme dateline.

**How to break it:**
Any screen without a temporal anchor. The reader should never have to ask "when is this."

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Term 4 — The Reader's Numbers Are Always Available</strong></summary>

From the moment the reader enters their first variable, their numbers travel with them through the simulation. Every screen that references that variable shows their number, not the mock number. This is not announced. It just happens.

**How to honor it:**
The variable system replaces mock data with real data silently. The article does not say "using your real numbers." It just uses them.

**How to break it:**
Any transition screen that acknowledges the switch from mock to real data. The switch should be invisible.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Term 5 — The Simulation Will Always Be Honest About Luck</strong></summary>

The simulation never implies that correct outcomes were inevitable or that incorrect outcomes were deserved. Vehicle 5 admits luck played a role. This admission is non-negotiable. It is the most important line in the simulation and the one that makes the reader trust everything that came before it.

**How to honor it:**
Write Vehicle 5's wins with the same restraint as everything else. Do not celebrate them. Do not editorialize. In Era 9 the admission lands harder because nothing before it oversold the outcome.

**How to break it:**
Any sentence that implies Vehicle 5's wins were the result of superior decision-making alone. They were not. The leverage variable and the luck variable are indistinguishable until after the fact. Write that honestly.

</details>

## 10. The Middle — The Pivot Points

These are the four moments where the emotional register shifts significantly. Each one must be written to carry the weight of everything before it while opening the door to everything after it.

<details>
<summary><strong>Pivot 1 — When Suspense Becomes Horror (Era 3, September 15 2008)</strong></summary>

**What changes:**
The thing the reader has been dreading arrives. Lehman files. The shift from anticipation to consequence is the pivot. Everything before this was dread. Everything after this is the cost of that dread being justified.

**How to write it:**
Write Lehman's collapse smaller than the reader expects. One sentence. Flat. Then show what happened to each vehicle that same day. The five simultaneous reactions are more devastating than any description of the event itself.

**The risk:**
Writing the event too large. The event is already enormous in the reader's mind. Do not compete with what they already know. Step aside and show the human consequences.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Pivot 2 — When Horror Becomes Opportunity (Era 4, March 9 2009)</strong></summary>

**What changes:**
The market bottoms. For the first time in the simulation a terrible moment is simultaneously the best possible moment to act. The emotional register shifts from horror to the specific agony of a window that most vehicles will not take.

**How to write it:**
Show the number. S&P 500 closes at 676. One sentence. Then show what one vehicle does with that number. Then nothing. Move to the next era. Let the reader sit with the $1,000 decision until Era 5 shows them what it became.

**The risk:**
Telling the reader this is the bottom. They already know. Do not say it. Write it as if it is just another day in a series of bad days. The reader's knowledge of what this day actually was is the entire emotional content.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Pivot 3 — When Patience Becomes Complacency (Era 6, December 2019)</strong></summary>

**What changes:**
The longest bull market in history has made the correct decisions feel like the only possible decisions. The reader has been watching things go right for six years. The specific danger of that comfort is the pivot.

**How to write it:**
The December 2019 news item. One line. No article. No link. Just the date and the sentence. "A novel pneumonia-like illness has been identified in Wuhan, China." Then the era ends. The reader carries that line into Era 7 alone.

**The risk:**
Cushioning it. Any sentence that follows that line softens it. Let it be the last thing on the screen. Let the reader advance to Era 7 carrying it.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>Pivot 4 — When History Becomes Personal (Era 8, 2021)</strong></summary>

**What changes:**
Inflation arrives. The reader has been living through this. The simulation is no longer historical. The variables are current. The reader's real numbers are in the app. This era is not about five vehicles anymore. It is about the reader.

**How to write it:**
Write Era 8 with slightly more intimacy than the other eras. Not much. Just enough to signal that the register has changed. The reader should feel the shift without being told it has happened.

**The risk:**
Overplaying it. The intimacy should be subtle — one or two sentences that feel slightly more direct than usual. The reader will feel it. Trust them.

</details>

## 11. The End — The Emotional Landing

The simulation ends once. It must land correctly. There is no second chance.

<details>
<summary><strong>What The Reader Must Feel</strong></summary>

Not inspired. Not motivated. Not proud of themselves for finishing.

**Clear.**

The specific clarity that comes from having watched the full consequence arc of decisions that are available to them right now. Not theoretical clarity. Felt clarity. The kind that is still present three weeks later when a financial decision comes up in real life and something in the reader's brain connects it to something they felt in the simulation.

That is the only outcome that matters. Everything in this document exists to produce that outcome.

</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>The Final Comparison</strong></summary>

Five vehicles. Same starting point. Eighteen years. Five outcomes.

This comparison must be written with absolute restraint. No celebration of the winner. No pity for the loser. No editorial comment on what any of it means. Just the numbers. And below the numbers — one honest line from Vehicle 5.

"Luck played a role. I know it."

Then nothing. The reader sits with all of it.

</details>

## 12. The Drift Checklist

Ask these six questions about every screen before it goes into the simulation. If any answer is wrong, rewrite before proceeding.

<details>
<summary><strong>The Six Questions</strong></summary>

**1. Does this screen tell the reader how to feel?**
If yes — remove the editorial language. Show the fact. Trust the reader.

**2. Does this screen contain a person where there should be a decision?**
If yes — remove the person. Replace with the variable or the decision. "One household moved savings" not "she decided to move her savings."

**3. Does this screen explain a concept before the reader has felt why it matters?**
If yes — reorder. Feel first. Explanation second. Always.

**4. Does this screen scroll?**
If yes — the content is too long. Cut until it fits one viewport. This is a writing constraint. Cut from the middle. The beginning and the end are the most important parts of any screen.

**5. Does this screen introduce a component or feature?**
If yes — remove the introduction. The component should simply be there. The reader discovers it. You do not announce it.

**6. Does this screen stay in tone?**
Identify which of the three tones this screen should be. Read it against the tone description. If it has drifted — identify the specific sentences that drifted and rewrite them.

</details>

## 13. The One Rule

<details>
<summary><strong>Read this before writing anything</strong></summary>

**The reader must always feel the consequence before they understand the mechanism.**

Everything in this document — every lever, every tone, every emotional register, every vehicle's function — exists to serve this one rule.

A burn rate is not interesting. Watching $7,000 become 97 days and then watching 97 days become 31 days and then watching 31 days become a decision made in panic — that is interesting. The mechanism is the same. The order of delivery is everything.

If a screen explains before it makes the reader feel, it has failed regardless of how well it is written. Rewrite it. Show the consequence first. The mechanism will mean something once the reader already cares about the outcome.

This is not a writing style. It is the entire pedagogy of the simulation. The reader becomes financially literate not because they were taught finance but because they felt the consequences of financial decisions until the knowledge attached itself to something real enough to stick.

Write every screen to produce a feeling first. The understanding will follow on its own.

</details>
