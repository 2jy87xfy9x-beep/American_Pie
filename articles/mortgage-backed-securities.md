## What It Is

A mortgage-backed security bundles thousands of home loans into bonds investors can trade. When homeowners pay principal and interest, cash flows through to bondholders — unless delinquencies spike and the models lie.

Wall Street called it innovation. Kitchen tables in 2008 called it “why my neighbors vanished.”

## The Math

If someone holds a $200k balance at 6.5% APR for illustration, monthly interest alone ≈ **$200,000 × 0.065 ÷ 12 ≈ $1,083** before principal paydown.

Scale that logic to trillions of pooled loans: when default rates bend upward, the expected cash flow to **MBS** holders shrinks — funds holding “safe” bond sleeves get marked down even if you thought you owned “diversified income.”

Against **{{your_monthly_expenses}}**, a surprise rent increase of even $150 is **$150 ÷ {{your_monthly_expenses}} × 100** percent of the monthly burn — painful precisely because **{{your_cash}}** only covers **{{coverage_days}}** days if income stops.

## What People Did

**What most people did**

They learned the acronym the Monday *after* the prime fund broke the buck — when fear, not curiosity, drove Wikipedia traffic.

**What financially stable people did**

They read fund fact sheets: what percentage was agency MBS versus private label, what duration meant for price drops when spreads blew out.

▸ If you had known this before 2008

You would not have smirked at subprime doc jokes on late-night TV while your 401k bond sleeve quietly held the exposure. You would have opened one PDF, seen the concentration, and rebalanced before margin calls became cocktail chatter.
