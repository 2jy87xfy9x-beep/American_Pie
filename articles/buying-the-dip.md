## What It Is

“Buying the dip” means purchasing assets after prices fall, betting the decline overreacts.

The trap: dips keep dipping while storytelling still feels clever — courage without **{{your_cash}}** math is leverage wearing a mask.

## Your Number

Cash available **{{your_cash}}** vs monthly obligations **{{your_monthly_expenses}}** decides whether a dip purchase is investing or gambling.

Investments marked **{{your_investment_value}}** show what you already exposed to sequence risk.

## The Math

Suppose you deploy **$2,000** when an index is **−30%** off highs but it falls another **−20%** before bottom. Weighted average entry improves versus peak buyers but **$2,000** still marks **−44%** peak-to-trough on that tranche emotionally.

Relative to **{{your_monthly_expenses}}**, that **$2,000** was **$2,000 ÷ {{your_monthly_expenses}}** months of runway you chose to convert to volatility.

If **{{coverage_days}}** < **60**, buying dips with rent money is not contrarian — it is liquidation risk with choreography.

## What People Did

**What most people did**

They bought once, watched more red, froze, then sold lower — converting temporary dip into permanent loss.

**What financially stable people did**

They sized buys as fractions of **{{your_cash}}** after reserving **N** months of **{{your_monthly_expenses}}**, used limit orders or calendar schedules, and refused margin.

▸ If you had known this before 2008

You would remember the nausea of catching a falling knife with **{{your_cash}}** you told yourself you “wouldn’t need.” The market did not care about your inner monologue — only your ledger did.
